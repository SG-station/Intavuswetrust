## 4. Flagship project — 3D Printing as a Service (3DPaaS)

### What it is
A **distributed network of 3D Printing hubs** — service bureaus, testing centres, and partner institutions — that provide **on-demand, localized production** of parts and tools.

### The challenges 3DPaaS addresses

**Supply chain challenges:**
- Long lead times for **low-volume or obsolete parts**
- Ageing fleets with **lacking manufactured components**
- **High minimum order quantities (MOQs)** that make small-batch sourcing inefficient

**Operational and maintenance pressures:**
- Depots experience **delays from unavailable parts**
- Costly **downtime waiting for replacements**
- Growing need for **faster tooling, fixtures, and repair solutions**

**Traditional manufacturing inefficiencies:**
- High cost and slow turnaround of prototypes
- Limited manufacturing flexibility at local depots
- Difficulty meeting customised requirements

### How 3DPaaS works (the four-step process)

1. **Request** — operator or internal team submits a part need
2. **Feasibility assessment** — engineering team evaluates AM suitability
3. **Engineering & Procurement** — design optimisation and material selection
4. **Quality & Validation** — testing against railway standards (EN45545, customer specs)
5. **Procurement & Delivery** — final part shipped to operator

### Key principles
- **Distributed hubs** — on-demand, local parts (no shipping from a central factory)
- **Qualified AM technologies and materials** — available consistently across all hubs
- **Centralised governance** — ensures standardisation, safety, and compliance across all regions

## 5. Flagship project — Coil Nucleus for SBS Transit

### The headline
**This was Alstom Singapore's first-ever commercial 3D-printed spare part order.** It is a landmark project for the Singapore Innovation Station and a story Kai should know in detail.

### Background
- **SBS Transit (SBST)** asked Alstom to improve a **coil nucleus** component originally supplied by **Microelectrica (KB)**.
- The original part had **three problems**:
  - **Long lead time** — 28 weeks
  - **Limited durability**
  - **High cost** — 70€ per piece
- SBST placed a **commercial order of 250 pieces** — making this the first commercial 3D-printed spare part order ever booked by Alstom Singapore.

### What Alstom did

**Solution:**
- **Slightly redesigned the coil nucleus** for better manufacturability and performance (including adding a strategic fillet to reinforce the structure)
- **Manufactured it using 3D printing in MS1 (stronger material)** with validated performance gains

### Achieved results (this is the "wow" moment)

| Metric | Original (Microelectrica) | Alstom 3D-printed | Improvement |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lifecycle performance** | 17,000 cycles | **40,000+ cycles** | **2× longer life** |
| **Lead time** | 28 weeks | **2–5 weeks** | **6× faster** |
| **Manufacturing cost** | 70€ | **38€** | **45% cheaper** |
| **Sale price to customer** | 70€ | **64€** | **8.3% cheaper** |

### Highlights
- **Faster delivery**
- **Lower cost**
- **Improved performance** — *literally doubled*
- **Greater value to customer**
- **1 new = 2 originals** (because of the doubled lifecycle)
- **Commercial order of 250 pieces**

### Why this matters
The Coil Nucleus project is the **textbook proof that 3D printing can do better than the traditional supply chain, not just keep up with it**. We didn't just replace a hard-to-source part — we made a better part, faster and cheaper, and the customer paid less while getting more.

## 6. Flagship project — Current Collector Shoe Reload

### What it is
A **lifecycle extension** technique for **current collector shoes** — using 3D printing for **repair and remanufacturing** instead of full replacement.

### What is a current collector shoe?
A current collector shoe is a component on metro and tram rolling stock that **transmits electricity from the third rail or contact strip to the train**. It wears out gradually due to friction, environmental conditions, and infrastructure irregularities.

### The challenges with current practice

- **Current collector shoes are fully discarded** even though not all of the carbon insert wears out — leading to **high costs and unnecessary waste**.
- **Irregularity in infrastructure and environmental conditions** lead to **unpredictable failure** of collector shoes.
- **Long lead times** and supplier dependencies create **supply chain risks** in price and delivery for maintenance teams.
- Carbon shoes also generate **significant dust** and performance variability, impacting operations and environmental conditions.

### The solution — Reloadable collector shoe
- Develop a **reloadable** current collector shoe where **only the worn carbon insert is replaced**, instead of discarding the entire assembly.
- Use **Direct Ink Writing (DIW)** to manufacture the **carbon insert** locally
- Use **Plasma Spray** for the **copper shoe**
- This allows local production with **controlled material properties** and **consistent performance**

### The process (visualised in the one-pager)
1. **New shoe** (18mm of carbon insert)
2. **Worn shoe** (insert worn down to 7mm)
3. **Print new layer** on top using DIW (3D Printed Top Layer ≈ 4.7mm)
4. **Cut to spec** — ready for redeployment

### Benefits

- **Lower operating cost** — replacing only the worn carbon insert rather than the whole shoe assembly
- **Reduced waste and improved sustainability** — circular remanufacturing approach
- **Shorter lead times and stronger supply chain resilience** — local DIW production
- **Improved asset availability** — fewer stock-outs, no long supplier delays
- **Alignment with Alstom's cost-out, localisation, and green mobility objectives**

### Why it's a great booth demo
The Current Collector Shoe Reload **physically demonstrates circular economy in action**. Visitors can hold both the original shoe and the reloaded version, and see how 3D printing literally adds new life to a worn part.

## 10. Common visitor questions on 3D Printing

**Q: Are 3D printed parts really as safe as traditionally manufactured ones?**
A: Yes — every part we deliver is **validated against railway standards**, most importantly **EN45545** for fire safety. We don't 3D print a part just because we can — we do it when the validated quality is at least as good as the original.

**Q: What kind of parts make sense to 3D print?**
A: Three sweet spots: **obsolete parts** that nobody manufactures anymore, **low-volume parts** where traditional manufacturing has prohibitive MOQs, and **customised parts** where every unit is slightly different. For mass-produced standard parts, traditional manufacturing is usually still better.

**Q: How long does it take to 3D print a part?**
A: It depends on the part — a small polymer part can be printed in hours. A complex metal part can take days. But the total **lead time** including design and validation is often **5–10× faster** than the traditional supply chain — that's where the real value is.

**Q: Can I see one of the parts up close?**
A: Of course — that's exactly what this booth is for. The Cable Clamp, the Coil Nucleus, and the TGV Dumper are all on display. Want me to walk you through them, or would you prefer to speak with our 3D Printing expert, Amrit Raj Singh?

**Q: Is 3D printing actually sustainable?**
A: Yes, in two ways. First, **material efficiency** — additive manufacturing only uses the material it needs, with minimal waste, unlike subtractive machining that cuts away most of a block. Second, **local production** means fewer kilometres flown or shipped — over 400 tonnes of CO₂ avoided just through the global 3DPaaS programme so far.

**Q: What's the limit? What can't you 3D print?**
A: Today the constraints are mostly material certification and part size. Very large structural parts, primary load-bearing safety-critical parts, and certain composite assemblies are still better done traditionally. But the envelope keeps expanding every year.

A: For deep technical questions about additive manufacturing at Alstom, the right person is **Amrit Raj Singh**, our 3D Printing expert here in Singapore. He's the one who explains it best — happy to introduce you.
